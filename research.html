When Does Visual Manipulation Work? Evidence from Online Marketplaces
Job Market Paper Under Review
Ali Kozehgaran, Liangbin Yang, Juncai Jiang
Under review at Marketing Science
Abstract

Visual manipulation is increasingly prevalent in digital marketplaces, yet its impact on market outcomes remains largely undocumented. We study this question using a unique longitudinal panel of 33,000 Airbnb properties in California, where hosts may manipulate listing images to influence consumer evaluation. Employing a state-of-the-art forensic detection framework that detects both AI-based and Photoshop-based visual manipulation, we estimate the effect of manipulation on property revenue using a matrix completion approach to construct counterfactual outcomes. The results reveal substantial heterogeneity across manipulation technologies. AI manipulation increases monthly revenue by 14.3% (approximately $650), whereas Photoshop editing has no measurable effect. Furthermore, effects are strongest when alternative information signals (e.g., Superhost badge or guest reviews) are limited, when images convey richer information (e.g., high-amenity and entire-property listings), and when manipulation is applied at moderate levels and targets amenity-related focal elements. Overall, our findings show that visual manipulation can meaningfully enhance marketplace performance, but only under specific technological and contextual conditions. These results offer actionable guidance for platforms governing visual content and for firms calibrating visual manipulation strategies in digital environments.

Disintermediation and Coalition Formation in The Traditional Taxi Industry
Dissertation Essay
Ali Kozehgaran, Scott Fay, Minjung Kwon
Preparing for submission to the Journal of Marketing Research
Abstract

This study examines the causal impact of mobile hailing technology adoption and coalition-based network integration on traditional taxi drivers' performance and efficiency in a mixed-supply transportation model. Using panel data of +50 million trips from over 5,800 drivers across six years in Chicago, we apply an interactive fixed effects (IFE) estimator to account for latent heterogeneity and staggered adoption, and validate our findings using the Callaway and Sant’Anna (2021) difference-in-differences framework. Our results show that platform adoption significantly increases driver performance—raising income, trips, and total working time—but these gains are primarily driven by greater labor input rather than improvements in operational efficiency. Critically, we find that affiliation with a network of platform partners (i.e., a coalition) does not, on its own, amplify the benefits of adoption. The largest and most sustained performance and efficiency gains emerge only when coalition membership is paired with high usage intensity. These findings point to a behavioral complementarity: the value of technology is contingent not just on adoption, but on sustained engagement within a structured, trusted network. Our analysis reveals that partial adoption or overreliance on platform-based dispatching can undermine spatial efficiency, while integrated users—those who are both network-affiliated and high-usage—are better positioned to mitigate these inefficiencies. These insights align with recent observations from other sectors, such as AI adoption, where lack of organizational integration and usage behavior often limits efficiency gains. By combining robust causal inference with a detailed behavioral and structural analysis, this study provides novel insight into the role of coalition design in enabling effective platform transformation. The findings offer practical guidance for platform managers, policymakers, and service providers seeking to foster sustainable innovation in traditional service markets.

Using Leasing Intensity to Signal Product Longevity
Under Review
Scott Fay, Ali Kozehgaran
Under review at the Journal of Quantitative Marketing and Economics (QME)
Abstract

Consumers’ willingness-to-pay for a durable good depends upon its perceived longevity, i.e., the duration for which the product continues to function properly. However, because consumers cannot observe longevity prior to purchase, it is difficult for manufacturers to capitalize on investments that enhance product longevity. In this paper, we demonstrate that the leasing market can provide a credible mechanism for signaling product longevity. In our analytical model, a manufacturer both leases and sells a durable good. Leased units are returned to the manufacturer after the lease period ends and then leased again in the secondary market as long as they continue to be functional. When consumers cannot observe longevity, a separating equilibrium arises in which a manufacturer signals high longevity by reducing the lease price and allocating a disproportionate share of production to the leasing market (i.e., uses a high “leasing intensity”). Consumers benefit from this credible signaling because lessees now incur lower usage costs and buyers now avoid overpaying for products with low longevity. In fact, consumers, on average, are better off in the signaling equilibrium than if longevity were directly observable. Our finding that signaling enhances manufacturers’ incentives to invest in product longevity suggests this could be an important mechanism for promoting sustainability initiatives.

Beyond the First Impression: A Two-Stage Signaling Framework for Consumer Decisions on Digital Platforms
Working Paper
Ali Kozehgaran, Meheli Basu, Liangbin Yang, Scott Fay
Abstract

Models how guests process both immediately visible signals (First Impression Signals) and deeper contextual cues (Ancillary Signals) when forming Airbnb booking intentions. Surface-level signals drive initial consideration, but ancillary signals such as review sentiment and policy transparency exert strong independent influence, challenging the dominant role of traditional reputation badges.
