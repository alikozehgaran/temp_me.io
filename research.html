When Does Visual Manipulation Work? Evidence from Online Marketplaces
Job Market Paper
Ali Kozehgaran, Liangbin Yang, Juncai Jiang
Under review at Marketing Science
Abstract

Visual manipulation is increasingly prevalent in digital marketplaces, yet its impact on market outcomes remains largely undocumented. We study this question using a unique longitudinal panel of 33,000 Airbnb properties in California, where hosts may manipulate listing images to influence consumer evaluation. Employing a state-of-the-art forensic detection framework that detects both AI-based and Photoshop-based visual manipulation, we estimate the effect of manipulation on property revenue using a matrix completion approach to construct counterfactual outcomes. The results reveal substantial heterogeneity across manipulation technologies. AI manipulation increases monthly revenue by 14.3% (approximately $650), whereas Photoshop editing has no measurable effect. Furthermore, effects are strongest when alternative information signals (e.g., Superhost badge or guest reviews) are limited, when images convey richer information (e.g., high-amenity and entire-property listings), and when manipulation is applied at moderate levels and targets amenity-related focal elements. Overall, our findings show that visual manipulation can meaningfully enhance marketplace performance, but only under specific technological and contextual conditions. These results offer actionable guidance for platforms governing visual content and for firms calibrating visual manipulation strategies in digital environments.

Disintermediation and Coalition Formation in the Traditional Taxi Industry
Dissertation
Ali Kozehgaran, Scott Fay, Minjung Kwon
Preparing for submission to Journal of Marketing Research

Examines the causal impact of mobile hailing adoption and coalition-based network integration on taxi driver performance using panel data of 50+ million trips from 5,800+ drivers across six years in Chicago. Platform adoption raises income and trips, but primarily through greater labor input. Coalition membership amplifies benefits only when paired with high usage intensity, pointing to a behavioral complementarity between technology adoption and sustained engagement.

Learning from Service Experience Ratings: Effects on Experience Quality, Credence Quality, and Customer Satisfaction
Published
Scott Fay, Ali Kozehgaran
Journal of Retailing, 2025

Develops a principal-agent model to analyze how service ratings influence employee effort and customer satisfaction. We identify conditions under which soliciting service ratings is profitable for a retailer — and find, surprisingly, that a retailer may not benefit from collecting ratings even if doing so were costless.

Using Leasing Intensity to Signal Product Longevity
Under Review
Scott Fay, Ali Kozehgaran
Under review at Journal of Quantitative Marketing and Economics

Demonstrates that the leasing market can credibly signal product longevity. A manufacturer signals high longevity by reducing the lease price and allocating a disproportionate share of production to leasing. Consumers benefit because lessees incur lower costs and buyers avoid overpaying for low-longevity products. Signaling also enhances manufacturers' incentives to invest in product longevity, promoting sustainability.

Beyond the First Impression: A Two-Stage Signaling Framework for Consumer Decisions on Digital Platforms
Working
Ali Kozehgaran, Meheli Basu, Liangbin Yang, Scott Fay

Models how guests process both immediately visible signals (First Impression Signals) and deeper contextual cues (Ancillary Signals) when forming Airbnb booking intentions. Surface-level signals drive initial consideration, but ancillary signals such as review sentiment and policy transparency exert strong independent influence, challenging the dominant role of traditional reputation badges.
